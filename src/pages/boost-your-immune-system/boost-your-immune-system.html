How Coconut Oil Functions

Among all natural plant oils, coconut oil is unique because of its high content of potent medium chain fatty acids (MCFAs) which are immune boosters. Significant MCFAs present in the oil, are capric acid, caprylic acid, and lauric acid.

To activate the anti-microbial properties that can destroy disease-causing organisms the oil has to be digested. After digestion, the medium chain fatty acids are broken down into free fatty acids and monoglycerides.

The individual fatty acids attack the microbes differently. Some MCFAs might be active in killing certain bacterial infections. Alternatively, they may be less active in destroying microbes. However, you still get an active immunity against diseases and other coconut oil benefits.

Fatty Acids in Coconut oil and their Functions

Caprylic Acid

It has essential natural antibacterial, anti-viral, anti-inflammatory and anti-fungal properties. It can create a hostile environment for viruses, reduce candida and fight bacteria.

Capric Acid

The fatty acid has strong antibacterial and antiviral properties. These make coconut oil able to fight viral and bacterial agents in the body. Capric acid can also help to improve your general wellbeing and overall health.

Lauric acid

Lauric acid is usually found in the breast milk, where aids the child's immune system. Coconut oil has a high content of lauric acid which has essential and natural anti-microbial properties. Therefore it can either inhibit the growth of microorganisms or kill them.

Lauric acid from coconut oil is part of an ongoing drug therapy program where is used to reduce the HIV infection. It does this by decreasing the viral load of the HIV patient.

Monolaurin is a chemical derivative of lauric acid commonly used as a dietary supplement. It can prevent and treat colds, herpes, shingles and other infections. Scientists are still finding ways to use the derivative to provide more coconut oil benefits.
